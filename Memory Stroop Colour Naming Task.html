Abstract

  • The Stroop colour naming task has been widely used to investigate the mechanisms underlying attentional bias, it has also been used to examine implicit memory.

  • Using the study-test procedure 60 native English speakers were tested to determine whether study effects from neutral words that had previously been studied would cause interference in colour naming.

  • The results showed that studied words had a tendency to slow down the studied block consisting of both studied and unstudied words compared to the unstudied block comprising of unstudied words only. The study effect was strong in the 1st half of the experiment and it diminished in the 2nd half of the experiment (See below Dashboard created in Tableau).

    1.

    Introduction

    This section introduces the Stroop colour naming task and its modified versions.

    Stroop Task using colour words

    In cognitive psychology, the Stroop colour naming task has been widely used to investigate selective attention.

    In the task, participants are presented with a set of words printed in colour, and the goal is to name the ink colour as soon as possible while ignoring the meaning of the word.

    When the ink colour doesn't match the meaning of the word (try below column 4), you'd likely take longer to name the ink colour (and be more likely to get it wrong) than when the ink colour matches the word (try below column 2).

    Stroop Task using using non-colour words with the Study-Test procedure

    Researchers have found that by using the Study-Test procedure in a trial-by-trial format, a study item immediately followed by a test item, participants showed a slowdown in colour naming when they responded to the non-colour words they have recently studied.

    For example (See below left panel), when you are given a set of 3 dog's breed-related words (Chihuahua, Husky, Bulldog) to study, then you are asked to name the ink colour of a test item. You are more likely to be slower when responding to the studied word 'Chihuahua' compared to the unstudied word 'textbook', which is a result of 'Study Effect'.

    Following the above research, an American psychologist, MacLeod, was curious whether this 'Study Effect' can still survive if the time gap between the 'Study' item and the 'Test' item becomes longer.

    So, he applied the Study-Test procedure in a block-by-block format to his experimental design. That is, participants studied a block of 12 neutral words (e.g., table, class, door...etc.), and then had a block of colour naming task which contained a mixture of the 12 studied and 12 unstudied words (See above right panel).

    However, the result of MacLeod's research did not show any study effect where RT to Studied words did not differ from RT to Unstudied words.

    2.

    Aim

    This section explains the purpose of this research project, and the questions this project aimed to solve.

    Rationale: This research project argued that studying words can affect naming colours during the blocked format colour naming task. Therefore, in MacLeod's experiment, it was possible the study effect occurred in the test block. But because MacLeod's design did not include a baseline block to compared with the test block, it was not possible to reveal the study effect.

    Experimental Design: This research project attempted to replicate MacLeod's design where there is a Study block followed by a Test block containing a mixture of Studied and Unstudied words (Mixed block). In addition, an extra Test block consisting of only Unstudied words (Pure block) was added to serve as a baseline (See below Figure).

    Questions to solve:

    • Within the Mixed block:

      • Is RT to the Studied words = RT to the Unstudied words, which is like what MacLeod found?

    • Between the Mixed and Pure blocks:

      • Is RT to the Mixed block > RT to the baseline Pure block?

      • Is RT to Unstudied-Mixed > RT to Unstudied-Pure?

    • If the study effect occurs in the Mixed block, does the amount of effect remain the same through the entire block?

    Experimental Design

    • A single factorial design was employed with Word type (Studied-mixed, Unstudied-mixed, Unstudied-pure) as the within-subject factor.

    • The dependent variable was the mean correct reaction time to name the ink colours the words were printed in.

    Apparatus and materials

    • Apparatus:

      • The experiment program was written in E-Prime 2.0 (Psychology Software Tools) and presented on a 60 cm Dell widescreen monitor.

      • Reaction time was measured during the colour naming tasks.

      • The manual responses, the presentation and randomisation of the words were controlled by E-Prime 2.0.

    • Marterials:

      • 100 neutral words were chosen from the English Lexicon Project and were divided into ﬁve lists of 20 words.

      • Each word set contained an equal number of 4, 5, 6 and 7 letter words and were matched for word frequency.

    Procedure

    • Each participant was given an information sheet and a consent form to sign upon arriving at the lab.

    • The participant was asked to read the instructions on the computer screen.

    • The experiment comprised two phases: the study phase and test phase.

      • Study phase: Each participant was shown 20 words one at a time and asked to do their best to memorise them for a later memory test.

      • Test phase: There were 2 test blocks containing 320 trials in total. Participants were presented with one word printed in colour on each trial, and asked to ignore the word and respond to ink colour as quickly and as accurately as possible.

    4.

    Data Cleaning

    This section presents the flow of preparing the dataset for the analysis.

    Once each participant completed the experiment, their data was stored in a CSV file. 60 CSV files were merged into 'Raw_data.csv', which contains 76 columns in total. The data was then filtered down to 12 columns that were necessary to create the dataset for aggregation.

    At the end, the dataset was aggregated to 6 columns for the analysis:

    • Subject: Each participant is recorded with a subject number.

    • ACC: The accuracy for each trail. The value '1' refers to correct response, whereas '0' refers to incorrect response.

    • Error: The error for each trail. Opposite to ACC.

    • Mean Correct Reaction Times (ms): Participants' reaction time to the trials where they correctly identified the ink colour.

      • The very 1st trial of each Test block will be removed since normally it takes longer time to respond at the beginning of the experiment.

      • Trials with reaction times faster than 300ms or slower than 3,000ms will be removed.

    • Word Type: 3 word types - Studied-mixed, Unstudied-mixed, and Unstudied-pure.

    • Trial_2bins/Block Half: If the Study effect occurred, I will look into 1st half & 2nd half of each Test block to see.

    Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)

    In this section, ANOVA will be used to examine whether there is any difference in participants' reaction time to colour between three word types (Studied-mixed, Unstudied-mixed, Unstudied-pure), and if the difference exists, we then look into the data by Trial_2bins/Block Half (1st half, 2nd half) to see whether the pattern of the 1st half differs from that of the 2nd half.

    What is ANOVA?

    ANOVA is a statistical test to analyse whether the means of three or more groups are different.

      ANOVA produces 2 values:

    • F-value - ANOVA assumes the means of all groups are the same, calculates how much the actual means deviate from the assumption, and reports it as the F-value.

      • A larger value means the difference between the means is larger.

    • p-value - A p-value is the probability when the null hypothesis is true for the F-values.

      • The null hypothesis assumes the means of all groups are equal.

      • In general, when p-value < 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis because this means there is less than 5% chance that the null hypothesis is true.

    If ANOVA suggests a significant difference between three word types, Paired Sample t-tests will be used to compare the means of any two groups to see where the difference exists.

    What is T-test?

    T-test is a statistical test to determine whether the means of two groups are different based on the p-value it produces.

    6.

    Key Findings and Conclusion

    The main purpose of this research project was to argue that that studying words can slow down the responses to ink colours in the blocked format colour naming task.

    My argument was supported by the following key findings:

    • When checking the RT for the entire block (See below Bar chart created in Tableau):

      • Within the Mixed block, RT to the Studied words = RT to the Unstudied words, which reproduced MacLeod's findings.

      • Between the Mixed and Pure blocks, RT to the Mixed block > RT to the baseline Pure block. Importantly, RT to Unstudied-mixed > RT to Unstudied-pure, which indicated the effect of studying words.

    • Interestingly, when looking into the RT by breaking down both Mixed and Pure blocks into 1st half & 2nd half (See below Bar chart created in Tableau):

      • The pattern of the 1st half is similar to that of the entire block.

      • Whereas the pattern of the 2nd half changed where RT to Unstudied-mixed = RT to Unstudied-pure, which suggested a reduced study effect compared to the 1st half.

    In conclusion, this research project provided evidence in support of using Study-Test procedure in the blocked format colour naming task can slow down participants' responses to ink colours.

    Key takeaway:

    The slowdown in colour-responding could be due to 'Task Conflict' between 'Colour naming task' and 'Word reading task'. This is because we are skilled readers in which when we seeing a word, we tend to read the word, which activates 'Word reading task'. This tendency to read word is stronger for recently attended words such as studied words. As a result, because of the study effect, despite participants were asked to ignore words and pay attention to colours, their attention was allocated to attend to the words.
